The difference between artifical intelligence and machine learning

Most people tend to use the terms artifical intelligence and machine learning interchangeably, especially people outside of the technological industry.

Artifical intelligence (AI) is simply a system that resembles human intelligence. Machine Learning (ML) is a system that is dependant on external data to learn. Thus, ML is a subset of AI.

Here is an example: Suppose we want to build a program that looks at a photo to see if it has a dog or not.

The AI way to do this is to hard-code what makes up a dog so the system knows. For example; does the dog have 4 legs, a tail, fur, and so on. The problem with this is that a picture could feature a dog-looking like thing (a 😺 for example) and the system would still think it's a dog.

With machine learning, we could "feed" the system lots and lots of pictures of dogs and dog-looking like things and tell them what actually is a dog and what is not a dog. This is how the system learns. For more complex things, the ML way is arguably far superior to the AI way.

There are three main types of machine learning training processes: Supervised, Unsupervised, and Re-informent learning.

Supervised | You actually give answers to what the training data is (from our example: dog, dog, no dog, dog, etc).

Unsupervised | You don't give the system "answers". This is used primarily to find outlier data, for dimensionality reduction (i.e. to lower the quality of training data but still keep it unique), or for clustering (grouping data together).

Re-inforcement | Learning like a child. You give the system lollies when it does something correctly and send it to Dagestan 2 3 years if it was bad.
